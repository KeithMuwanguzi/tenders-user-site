Section 01Case title

This case study examines the route Inspire Care Outreach Ltd took onto Dorset Council's Care, Support, Housing and Community Safety Open Framework, where the provider secured a place on five lots in a single application. The procurement carried reference DN548158 and was run by the Council's Commercial and Procurement Team based at County Hall, Colliton Park, Dorchester. The outcome letter reached the provider on 06 September 2024.

Section 02Organisation type and service setting

The Contracting Authority is Dorset Council, a unitary authority commissioning a wide-spectrum care, support, housing and community safety framework that spans home-based and live-in care across multiple cohorts. The Open Framework architecture is structurally distinct from a closed-window framework, in that providers can apply at any point during the framework lifetime and successful providers receive Call-Off Opportunities through the e-tender system as new commissioning needs are released against each lot.

The framework's lot structure spans cohorts and service types, with Lot 1 covering Care and Support at Home (generalist domiciliary care), Lot 2 covering Live in Care (24-hour live-in support with rest-break and statutory cover arrangements), Lot 9 covering Support at Home for Learning Disabilities, Physical Disabilities and Autism, Lot 10 covering Support at Home for Mental Health and Autism, and Lot 11 covering Support at Home for Bespoke and Specialist services, including PEG feeding, ventilator support and complex medication regimes. The cohort scope across the framework is correspondingly wide, and includes older adults with frailty, dementia and progressive long-term conditions, adults with learning disability and autism (including high-acuity sensory and behavioural profiles), adults with physical disability requiring waking-night and 24-hour live-in support, adults with mental health needs including dual diagnosis and forensic step-down, and adults with bespoke specialist needs including end-of-life and palliative home care.

Section 03Client profile at entry

Inspire Care Outreach Ltd is an ambitious home-based care provider operating across multiple cohorts and aiming for county-wide credentialled status in Dorset. The organisation arrived at engagement start with CQC registration, current insurance and policy arrangements in place, and operational activity across most of the lots being applied for, with documented capability across home care, live-in care, learning disability, autism, mental health and bespoke specialist support.

The internal bid function had limited prior framework experience at this scale. The corporate evidence pack existed in working form, but the submission discipline of treating each lot as its own evidence problem, separate from the corporate backbone, was not yet embedded in the provider's bid practice, and that gap is the most common driver of multi-lot eligibility losses on frameworks of this kind.

Section 04Starting gaps and limitations

The first read of the application surfaced the most common multi-lot failure mode in the sector, in which a single set of generic answers is stretched across five distinct cohort lots. This profile produces eligible submissions on the more familiar lots and silent failures on the specialist lots, where cohort-specific evidence has to do the lift on the panel side. Live-in care evidence had not been separated from domiciliary care evidence, even though Lot 2 needs 24-hour cover protocols, statutory rest-break arrangements under the Working Time Regulations 1998, and continuous cover handover discipline as discrete evidence.

Mental health and autism content sat alongside learning disability content without lot-specific cohort detail, even though Lot 10 needs trauma-informed practice, Care Programme Approach familiarity and crisis de-escalation as discrete evidence, and bespoke specialist content needed named clinical interventions, such as PEG feeding, ventilator support and complex medication, rather than implied capability. Corporate insurance, policies and CQC registration were current but had not been packaged for evaluator consumption per lot, and the mandatory Declaration to the Chief Executive of Dorset Council was a procedural pass-fail that needed verification before submission.

Section 05Trigger for engagement

The provider needed access to Dorset Council referral routes across the widest possible service spread, and the Open Framework was the only structured route to bid on Council Call-Off Opportunities. Without entry on the relevant lots, the provider could not be invited to the procurements that would follow, and the brief on engagement was therefore direct: secure entry on as many lots as the operational evidence supports, do not lose any of them to a generic answer, and leave behind a corporate evidence backbone that could be reused for adjacent unitary and county procurements.

Section 06Risk position at the outset

The risk on this engagement was not eligibility on Lot 1 or Lot 2, the more familiar territory for a home-based care provider. The risk was eligibility loss on Lot 9, Lot 10 and Lot 11, where the cohort-specific evidence had to be sharper than the corporate boilerplate, because multi-cohort applications routinely fail on the specialist end when the same answer is reused across distinct evaluation criteria. Below the cohort risk, a procedural pass-fail miss on the Declaration to the Chief Executive, an out-of-date insurance certificate, or a current policy referencing superseded legislation would have closed individual lot routes before any cohort scoring engaged at all.

Section 07Constraints

The Open Framework operated on essential criteria thresholds rather than weighted quality scoring at framework entry. Each lot had to clear its essential criteria, and a single failure removed that lot from consideration. The mandatory Declaration to the Chief Executive was a procedural pass-fail, insurance, policies and CQC registration evidence had to be current and aligned to the lot scope, and the framework remained live throughout its duration with communications continuing automatically through the e-tender system. The clock was less aggressive than a closed-window framework, but the granularity of the lot evidence was higher.

Section 08Our role and level of involvement

We took the engagement as end-to-end lead author and submission coordinator. That covered the company information pack, lot-specific eligibility evidence, the Declaration confirmation, and the technical and operational evidence supporting each named cohort. The provider supplied operational facts, named cohort leads, clinical pathways, live-in cover protocols and specialist intervention evidence, and we turned them into a discrete eligibility submission per lot, sharing a single corporate evidence backbone.

Section 09Stabilisation actions

Three actions ran first, and the order reflected the architecture of an Open Framework application, in which eligibility evidence has to be current and lot-specific before any narrative is written. The corporate evidence pack was refreshed, with insurance certificates aligned to each lot's risk profile, policies tested for currency and references to superseded legislation, CQC registration confirmation against the public profile, and registered manager status confirmed against the application or current registration record.

Each lot's essential criteria was mapped against the operational evidence the provider could substantiate, and where a lot demanded a cohort-specific operational anchor that was not yet in evidence form, the supporting documentation was built before the answer was written. Live-in cover evidence was separated into its own bundle, including 24-hour cover protocols, named coordination roles, statutory rest-break arrangements under the Working Time Regulations 1998 and continuous cover handover discipline, so that Lot 2 could be treated as a distinct submission problem rather than as an extension of Lot 1.

Section 10Approach to building capability

We treated each lot as a discrete eligibility submission and matched the granularity of evidence to the granularity of the eligibility criteria, with the corporate evidence pack sitting behind every lot as the shared backbone. Each lot received cohort-specific operational evidence rather than generic adult social care content. Lot 9 was anchored on learning disability, physical disability and autism with named pathways. Lot 10 was anchored on mental health and autism with trauma-informed practice and Care Programme Approach familiarity. Lot 11 was anchored on bespoke specialist support with named clinical interventions, in which each clinical pathway, including PEG feeding, ventilator support and complex medication, was tied to a competency framework, a named lead and a verifiable training pathway.

Section 11How delivery was executed in practice

The provider's domiciliary care service was described as a coordinator-led, electronic call monitored, eMAR-supported, person-centred home care service with live-in capacity and specialist packages, and each cohort had a named lead and a documented pathway from referral through assessment, support planning, delivery and review. The narrative held inside the regulatory perimeter for each cohort rather than slipping into adult social care defaults. Mental health content referenced Care Programme Approach familiarity, autism content referenced sensory awareness and trauma-informed practice, and specialist content referenced named clinical interventions with competency evidence. Each operational paragraph was anchored to a verifiable system or named role.

Section 12Key interventions that changed trajectory

Three interventions did the work and each is repeatable on any multi-lot framework application. Cohort separation prevented the boilerplate failure mode, with each lot reading as a cohort-specific submission rather than as an extension of the corporate brochure. Live-in cover packaging treated Lot 2 as a distinct submission problem, with 24-hour cover, rest-break arrangements and continuous cover handover all evidenced separately, which protected the Lot 2 eligibility. Specialist anchoring named the clinical interventions, the named leads and the competency evidence, and Lot 11 cleared eligibility on the basis of named operational evidence rather than implied capability.

Section 13Outcome achieved

All five lots were awarded. Inspire Care Outreach Ltd is now an approved Dorset Council Open Framework provider for Lots 1, 2, 9, 10 and 11, with direct access to all Call-Off Opportunities released against any of these lots over the framework lifetime. The application cleared the Open Framework on first submission.

Section 15Before and after position

Before the Engagement

The provider was not approved on Dorset Council's Open Framework and had no structured route to bid for Council Call-Off Opportunities, which left it dependent on direct private placements within the Dorset footprint. Cohort-specific evidence had not been packaged per lot, and live-in cover was being treated as an extension of domiciliary care rather than as a discrete evidence problem.

After the Engagement

The provider is approved on five lots covering home care, live-in care, learning disability, autism, mental health and bespoke specialist support, and is eligible for Call-Off Opportunity invitations over the framework lifetime through the e-tender system. The corporate evidence backbone is now reusable across adjacent county and unitary procurements.

Section 16Client confidence and capability post-engagement

The provider exited the engagement holding a corporate evidence backbone and a lot-by-lot eligibility template that can be reused across future Open Frameworks and closed-window multi-lot frameworks. The shift in confidence was structural: where the team had previously over-written every answer with generalised material, there is now a discipline of matching the granularity of evidence to the granularity of the criteria. The internal team can read a multi-cohort framework and split it into discrete cohort submissions on first reading, and the discipline of treating live-in cover as a distinct submission problem and treating specialist capability as named operational evidence rather than implied breadth has been embedded into the provider's bid practice.

Section 18Repeat or ongoing work

The framework remains live throughout its duration, and the provider has retained ongoing support for Call-Off Opportunities released against each lot. The corporate evidence backbone is now reused across adjacent county and unitary authority applications, with cohort-specific lot templates being applied on procurements across the South West and beyond, and the provider's bid practice has shifted from one-off applications to a continuously maintained corporate evidence pack that supports rolling Open Framework activity.

Section 19Similar scenarios this applies to

The pattern recurs on Open Frameworks across multiple unitary authorities and on closed-window multi-lot frameworks where cohort-specific evidence drives eligibility.

  • Local authority Open Frameworks with rolling application windows.
  • Multi-lot care, support and housing frameworks.
  • Domiciliary, live-in and specialist tenders combined into a single procurement.
  • Frameworks with essential criteria gating before any quality scoring.
  • Tenders requiring named cohort experience for learning disability, autism, mental health and bespoke specialist services.
  • Frameworks requiring statutory rest-break arrangements under the Working Time Regulations 1998 for live-in lots.

Section 20Outcome summary

Five-lot Open Framework win on Dorset Council's flagship care procurement, with the provider entering the framework on its first application with a full evidence pack, lot-by-lot eligibility mapping and cohort-specific operational anchors. The provider now writes to cohort granularity rather than to a single corporate answer stretched across five distinct evaluation criteria, with reusable infrastructure that drives adjacent county and unitary authority submissions.
