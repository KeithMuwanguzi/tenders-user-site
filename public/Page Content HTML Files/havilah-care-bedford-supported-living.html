Source: Bedford Borough Council Intention to Award letter dated 11 July 2025, reference BBC AD 03786, Find a Tender 2025/S 000-002884, signed by Nadine Raenke, Senior Commissioning Officer (Adults), Business Transformation and Organisational Development.

Section 01Case title

This case study examines the route Havilah Care Ltd took onto Bedford Borough Council's Supported Living and Community Based Support Services Framework, which carried reference BBC AD 03786 and Find a Tender notice 2025/S 000-002884. The procurement was run under the Public Contract Regulations 2015 (as amended) by the Council's Business Transformation and Organisational Development team. The Intention to Award letter reached the provider on 11 July 2025, the standstill ran from 12 to midnight 21 July 2025, and the Council recorded that 154 tender submissions had been received against 44 successful awards.

Section 02Organisation type and service setting

The Contracting Authority is Bedford Borough Council, a unitary authority commissioning supported living and community-based support for adults under the Care Act 2014. The framework was procured under the Public Contract Regulations 2015 (as amended), with Lot 1 weighted 60 percent quality and 40 percent price, and Lot 2 weighted 100 percent quality, reflecting the Council's expectation that community-based support would be evaluated on quality alone within the published rate card.

The setting covers single-tenancy supported living, shared houses, outreach support and community-based domiciliary support delivered into the person's home, split across Lot 1 (Standard and Complex) and Lot 2 (community-based support). The pricing schedule covered Day Hours and 1-to-1 cover, Waking Night cover and Sleep-in cover across Standard and Complex categories, with maximum hourly rates published at £20.40 Standard, £21.80 Complex and £12.00 Sleep-in. The cohort scope across Standard and Complex includes adults with learning disability (including profound and multiple learning disability), adults with autism spectrum conditions including high-acuity sensory and behavioural profiles, adults with mental health needs (including dual diagnosis), adults with physical disability requiring waking-night and sleep-in cover, adults with co-occurring conditions requiring complex multi-disciplinary input, and adults with positive behavioural support plans and restrictive practice protocols.

Section 03Client profile at entry

Havilah Care Ltd is a growing adult social care provider building presence in supported living and community-based support, owner-led, with operational maturity in safeguarding and personalisation, and with a clear ambition to step into a Council-set framework that would bring referral pipeline and contractual scale. The arithmetic of the framework was decisive: Bedford received 154 submissions for 44 places, which is a sub-30 percent acceptance rate, and most rejected submissions on a procurement of this kind fall on the case-study gateway, where the Authority operates a binary 50 of 50 mark structure tied to sub-point criteria.

The provider arrived at engagement start with strong operational substance, a confident pricing posture and a draft narrative that needed gateway-level sub-point structuring before it would clear the case study cutoff. Without 50 of 50 on each case study, no quality scoring would engage on the rest of the bid, and that single arithmetic point dictated the order in which the engagement had to be run.

Section 04Starting gaps and limitations

The case studies were the binary gateway, and the provider's first draft included a confident overview of independent living skills work but had not yet tied every sub-point of the question criteria back to a worked example. Service planning detail on rota structure, screening, assessment workflow and capacity assurance was implied through the narrative rather than stated explicitly, and business continuity stress testing was thin: the BC risk areas had not been enumerated and the stress-testing methodology was not described. Assistive technology evidence carried headline references but did not show the panel any enabling detail or hour-reduction examples, cost-effectiveness through community resources was implied via independence outcomes rather than stated explicitly, and active service user roles in service design were under-evidenced, with coproduction referenced rather than demonstrated.

Each gap is a deduction waiting to happen on a 50 of 50 sub-point rubric, and combined they would have produced a case study at 35 to 40 of 50, which would have closed the framework door regardless of the strength of the quality questions that followed. The Q1 Service Provision evaluator commentary later named these gaps explicitly, citing rota and staff approach, BC stress testing, AT detail, agency working in emergencies, and active coproduction roles.

Section 05Trigger for engagement

The provider engaged us to clear the case study gateway at full marks and to maximise the quality score in the cohort lots that followed. Without 50 of 50 on each case study, no quality scoring would proceed, and without proportionate quality scoring, even a strong commercial position would not have produced both Standard and Complex acceptance. The strategic objective was a place on Lot 1 in both categories and a place on Lot 2.

Section 06Risk position at the outset

One missed sub-point on either case study would close the door, and six quality questions sat behind that door, each of which could lift or sink the total weighted score. Pricing had to land inside the published hourly rate ceilings without breach, and the Public Contract Regulations 2015 standstill from 12 to midnight 21 July 2025 made post-submission corrections impossible. Below the top-line risk, the framework's evaluator commentary structure was published per question, which meant any weakness would be read on record by the provider and by the procurement team, and a weak quality response is recoverable through commercial strength on Lot 1 but not on Lot 2, where quality is 100 percent of the score and there is no commercial backstop.

Section 07Constraints

The provider was working to two case studies (50 marks each), six quality questions, two scoring weights (60 percent quality on Lot 1 and 100 percent quality on Lot 2), and three pricing categories per lot covering Day Hours and 1-to-1, Waking Night and Sleep-in. The social value evidence base was active but lacked a quantified commitments tab, the training matrix existed in the operation but had not been turned into a one-page reference, and coproduction language was generalised rather than role-specific. Compliance documentation was due before contract draft, including insurance, contract manager and provider portal contact details, a signed letter-headed paper with bank and VAT details and a copy of insurance certificates clearly showing levels of Employer's Liability, Public Liability and Professional Indemnity, all by 21 July 2025.

Section 08Our role and level of involvement

We took the engagement as lead writer on case studies, every quality method statement, the partnership narrative, the pricing schedule, the social value commitments tab and the Plain Meaning Grading audit before submission. The provider supplied operational facts and pricing posture. We turned both into a hundred-percent-of-available-points submission on the gateway, and into a quality narrative aligned to the evaluator's published banding rules on the questions that followed.

Section 09Stabilisation actions

Three stabilisation actions ran first, and the order reflected the binary nature of the gateway, since a missing sub-point on a case study could not be recovered by a strong quality question downstream. Both case studies were restructured against every sub-point of the Authority's question criteria, with each sub-point anchored to a dated, named operational example, and adjustment to practice and risk-enabled resilience-building were threaded through the Complex case study. Where the published criteria asked for multi-disciplinary working, the example showed multi-disciplinary working with named professional roles and an outcome.

The social value commitments tab was packaged against measurable targets including apprenticeships, environmental commitments (energy efficiency, low-emission fleet, recyclable materials, sustainable sourcing), supply chain practice and named local partners including a local food bank, and the commitments tab was reconciled with the method statement so that no commitment appeared in one document and not the other. The multi-disciplinary and resilience narrative was written for the Complex case study around adjustment to practice, with strategies to support risk management and build resilience demonstrated through worked steps rather than referenced as concepts.

Section 10Approach to building capability

We took the case studies first because they were the gate, then the quality questions, structured around the evaluator's distinction between adequate, good and excellent levels. Q5 Partnerships received the densest treatment because it carried the highest available uplift on this provider's evidence profile, and pricing was calibrated last against the published price matrix without breach.

The 50 of 50 case study mark is awarded against sub-point completeness rather than against general fluency, and the provider's existing draft addressed several sub-points strongly while addressing others by implication. The rewrite tied every sub-point to a specific worked example, removed implication and protected the binary mark. The Authority's published banding distinguished acceptable (most requirements met), good (each element met with good detail) and excellent (all requirements met with high accuracy), and each question was written to the banding the evidence base could support rather than above it. Q5 Partnerships was written to excellent, Q1 Service Provision and Q2 Quality and Safeguarding were written to good with explicit improvement carry-forward against named gaps, and the pricing schedule was triple-checked against the matrix, with Day Hours and 1-to-1 priced at £20.40 Standard and £21.80 Complex (matrix ceiling), Waking Night priced at £20.40 Standard and £21.80 Complex, and Sleep-in priced at £12.00, which delivered the maximum sub-weighting on the published banding for Sleep-in cover.

Section 11How delivery was executed in practice

The submission described supported living and community-based support delivered through a registered manager, deputy manager and team leader structure, with positive behavioural support plans, named keyworkers, weekly keywork sessions, multi-disciplinary review cycles, monthly outcomes reporting and safe staffing ratios that were stress-tested through business continuity planning. Person-centred outcomes were evidenced through case examples that mapped onto the case study sub-points rather than running parallel to them.

Q5 Partnerships landed on excellent, with the narrative referencing joined-up working with health (community learning disability teams, mental health crisis pathways, primary care), housing (registered providers and local authority housing allocation), voluntary sector partners (advocacy, peer support, the named local food bank), and the wider Bedfordshire commissioning architecture. Coproduction was operational, with named service user roles in service review and feedback cycles.

Section 12Key interventions that changed trajectory

Three interventions did the heavy lifting on the result. The case studies were rebuilt sub-point by sub-point against the published criteria, each sub-point closed with a worked example, and the 50 of 50 mark was secured on both Standard and Complex Bespoke case studies, which opened the door to quality scoring on the rest of the bid. Q5 Partnerships was rebuilt around joined-up working across health, housing and voluntary sector partners, which earned an excellent rating and the maximum 15 percent contribution to total score, with the published feedback recording no improvement points on this question.

The pricing schedule was triple-checked against the matrix, and the resulting commercial scores reached 35.29 percent on Lot 1 Standard and 35.53 percent on Lot 1 Complex against an available 40 percent. The arithmetic was the difference between a confirmed entry and a deferred decision through Council clarification correspondence.

Section 13Outcome achieved

Both Standard and Complex categories were accepted on Lot 1 and Community Based Support was accepted on Lot 2. Total scores reached 69.04 percent on Lot 1 Standard, 69.28 percent on Lot 1 Complex and 56.25 percent on Lot 2, with an excellent rating recorded on Q5 Partnerships. The standstill closed at midnight on 21 July 2025 without challenge, and the compliance documentation was issued thereafter.

Section 15Before and after position

Before the Engagement

The provider had no place on Bedford's supported living framework and no route to Council referrals for Standard or Complex packages, and 110 of 154 applicants were rejected from this framework cycle. The sub-point case study structure had not yet been applied to the bid material, the social value commitments tab had not been quantified, and coproduction language was generalised rather than role-specific.

After the Engagement

The provider sits among the 44 successful providers from 154 submissions, with both Standard and Complex categories accepted on Lot 1 and Community Based Support accepted on Lot 2. Documented evaluation feedback identifies excellent partnerships and a clean improvement plan for staffing matrix detail, BC stress-testing, AT enabling examples and Bedford-specific social value localisation.

Section 16Client confidence and capability post-engagement

The provider exited the engagement holding two artefacts that did not exist as bid material at engagement start: a sub-point case study template and a partnership narrative that scored an excellent. Each is reusable on the next bid without rebuild from zero. The shift in confidence moved from writing to a generic supported living brief to writing to evaluator-published banding rules, and the internal team can now read the difference between an adequate, a good and an excellent response on first pass and rewrite where the evidence base does not yet support the higher band. The published evaluator commentary on this submission was used directly as the input to the next bid cycle, with named gaps converted into a documented improvement plan.

The Council received 154 tender submissions; 44 submissions were successful; successful bidders were scored in line with the published evaluation criteria and minimum scoring requirements detailed in the Invitation to Tender (ITT)."

Section 18Repeat or ongoing work

The framework is live across the contract term. The provider has retained ongoing support for adjacent supported living procurements where similar case-study gateways operate, including across the East of England and the wider Midlands footprint, and the improvement plan captured from Bedford's feedback feeds the next regional submission directly. The provider's bid practice has shifted from one-off submission preparation to a continuous improvement cycle anchored in published evaluator commentary, which is the form of bid maturity that compounds over multiple framework cycles.

Section 19Similar scenarios this applies to

The pattern recurs across competitive supported living frameworks where case studies operate as a binary gateway and quality scoring drives the bulk of the total mark.

  • Local authority supported living frameworks with case-study selection gates.
  • Standard and Complex tier supported living tenders.
  • Tenders combining supported living (Lot 1) with community-based support (Lot 2).
  • High-volume frameworks with 100 or more applicants and sub-30 percent acceptance rates.
  • Tenders weighted 60/40 quality versus price with hourly rate ceilings.
  • Pricing matrices with sub-weighted Day Hours, Waking Night and Sleep-in components.

Section 20Outcome summary

One of 44 successful providers from 154 submissions, with full marks on both selection case studies, an excellent rating on Partnerships, and all three target lots and tiers accepted under a single framework agreement. The provider's internal bid practice now reads to the evaluator's banding rules rather than to a generic supported living narrative, and the documented improvement map structured directly from the published evaluator commentary now sits at the front of the next regional bid cycle.
