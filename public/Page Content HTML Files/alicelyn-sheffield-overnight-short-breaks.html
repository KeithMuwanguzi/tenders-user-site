Section 01Case title

This case study examines the entry of Alicelyn Healthcare onto Sheffield City Council's Adults with Disabilities Dynamic Purchasing System for Lot 3 Overnight Short Breaks. The tender carried reference 62329 and was procured by the Council's Finance and Commercial Services function under Public Contract Regulations 2015. The provisional award was communicated to the provider on 24 January 2025 by Successful Alcatel Letter, with the standstill period running until midnight on 3 February 2025 in accordance with Regulation 87.

Section 02Organisation type and service setting

The Contracting Authority is Sheffield City Council, a metropolitan unitary authority commissioning adult social care across the Sheffield travel-to-work area. Rather than running a closed framework with a single application window, the Council operates a Dynamic Purchasing System for adult social care, which allows it to maintain a continuously refreshed pool of qualified providers and to direct individual placements through call-off as referrals arrive.

Lot 3 sits inside that DPS architecture as the dedicated route for Overnight Short Breaks. The setting is regulated overnight respite for adults with disabilities, structured as planned, time-limited stays. A successful Lot 3 provider has to be able to hold a person safely overnight in a sensory-aware environment, sustain a consistent bedtime routine, deliver waking-night cover where the assessment indicates it is needed, manage continence and medication overnight, and re-engage the person into their daytime routine the following morning. Short breaks of this kind are commissioned in part to keep family carers in their caring role, because the deterioration trajectory that follows carer breakdown is precisely what the Care Act 2014 eligibility duties exist to interrupt.

Section 03Client profile at entry

Alicelyn Healthcare arrived at engagement start with a CQC registration in place, an established residential and respite footprint in Yorkshire, and the operational maturity of an owner-led adult social care business that had been delivering on the ground for several years. The senior team was experienced in care delivery, and the organisation held the kind of working knowledge of overnight cover patterns that does not always make it into a tender response in a form that an evaluator can mark.

What the provider did not have at engagement start was a dedicated bid lead, a consolidated case-example library, or a written response architecture that translated existing operational practice into evaluator-grade evidence. Bid writing capacity sat with the same operational management who were running the service through the same week, so the time available for response writing was limited and the appetite for procedural risk was low.

Section 04Starting gaps and limitations

When we read the provider's draft narrative for the first time, three structural gaps became visible quickly. None of them reflected weakness in the underlying operation; they reflected the fact that operational practice had not yet been turned into evaluator-grade material.

The shift cover description was written in informal language that did not show the panel a clear picture of overnight ratios. Waking-night and sleep-in arrangements were referenced in narrative form, but they had not been mapped onto a one-page staffing matrix with named roles, escalation triggers and documented ratio rules. Because overnight is the operationally distinctive feature of Lot 3, an evaluator who cannot find that detail in the first paragraph of the workforce response tends to read the answer as a competency risk, even when the provider is in fact running a strong overnight operation in practice.

The case examples were written as outcome stories rather than as evaluator structures. Identification, intervention, outcome, lessons learned and policy change were all present in the underlying incident records, but they had not been sequenced inside the prose in a way that would let a panel mark each beat. The fifth beat, the one that documents how a lesson actually changed the provider's policy or procedure, is the beat most often missed in submissions of this kind, and it costs marks that the case example would otherwise have earned.

The safeguarding and Mental Capacity Act content leaned on policy text rather than on a worked, dated example tied to the Sheffield Safeguarding Adults Board referral pathway. A response that quotes the legislation correctly but does not show the legislation being used in practice reads, on the page, as compliant rather than capable, and the marking band moves accordingly.

Section 05Trigger for engagement

The DPS notice landed against an operational backdrop in which Sheffield's overnight short breaks pathway is a recurring referral source for the city's commissioning team. Exclusion at the first window would have meant the provider could not be invited to any Council-led overnight respite call-off until the next refresh, which is a position the organisation could not afford to absorb without ceding referral pipeline and city-level visibility to competing providers. The brief on engagement was therefore direct: prepare the submission to evaluator standard, secure the place at the first window, defend it through standstill, and leave behind the kind of bid infrastructure that would carry the provider through subsequent call-offs and into adjacent procurements across South Yorkshire and West Yorkshire.

Section 06Risk position at the outset

Two failure modes were live at engagement start, and they sat at different stages of the evaluation flow. The first was a pass-fail eligibility miss on selection criteria, which would close the route before any qualitative scoring engaged at all. A missing CQC location confirmation, a missed registered manager declaration, an out-of-date ICO registration reference, or a policy citation referring to superseded legislation would all close the door without an evaluator ever reading the technical content of the bid.

The second failure mode was qualitative drift. A technically eligible bid that scored at the floor of acceptable would secure a place on the DPS, but it would expose the provider to ranking risk on call-offs, where Lot 3 operates with comparative scoring across the DPS pool. Because Public Contract Regulations 2015 Regulation 87 standstill provided a window in which unsuccessful bidders could challenge the award, the response had to be defensible on its face from the moment it landed in the evaluator's hands, and a thinly evidenced narrative is a structurally weak position to defend.

Section 07Constraints

The clock was the dominant constraint on this engagement. The submission window did not accommodate operational re-engineering of any kind, and the internal capacity available to support the bid was constrained by the fact that the same managers who would have to brief the writer were the people running the overnight shifts on the floor. The provider's electronic homecare management system and incident reporting system were both active in operation, but their outputs had not yet been turned into bid-grade evidence, and the CQC registered manager evidence was current but scattered across HR records, the CQC public profile and internal policy folders rather than collated into a single confirmation pack. Each of these is a manageable constraint individually, and together they shaped the order in which the engagement had to be run.

Section 08Our role and level of involvement

We took the submission as an end-to-end, sole-author engagement. That covered the specification reading, the response architecture, every method statement, the overnight staffing and cover narrative, the safeguarding and risk content, the policy mapping and the final compliance check before upload to the Council's e-tender portal. The provider supplied the operational facts behind every claim, including shift patterns, training content, eMAR usage, named registered manager, named senior staff identified by initials in line with information governance practice, and three live incident extracts that became the seeds for the case examples in the response. Our job was to translate those facts into evaluator-grade evidence and to leave behind reusable infrastructure that would carry the provider into the next bid window.

Section 09Stabilisation actions

Three stabilisation actions ran before any draft prose was rewritten, and the order mattered, because each removed a category of risk that the writing process would otherwise have had to carry. We read the specification line by line and produced a one-page weighting register that listed every assessment criterion, the marks available, the cohort terminology the Council used, the hard gates around CQC and registered manager status, and the named overnight cover expectations. That register became the binding constraint on every paragraph that followed, and it stopped the writing from drifting into adjacent topics that the evaluator was not asking about.

We pulled the provider's overnight staffing pattern into a single one-page matrix that named the sleep-in to waking-night escalation triggers, confirmed the twilight cover and morning re-engagement timings, and was structured for an evaluator's reading rather than for an internal rota planner's. Finally, we worked with the provider to rebuild three live incidents as five-beat case examples, each with dated identification, intervention, outcome, lessons learned and policy change, and we coded each example to the question and weighting band where it would do the most work.

Section 10Approach to building capability

The TenderLab method ran through four stages, and each stage produced an artefact the provider could keep and reuse, which is the difference between writing a bid for a client and growing the client's bid capability. The specification extraction stage produced the weighting register and a short reading note summarising the lot scope, the cohort, the weighting and the named risk areas. The response architecture stage anchored every method statement against the Council's question structure, so that an evaluator working through the scoring sheet could find every assessment point in the same order that it appeared on the page.

The operational proof stage embedded the provider's overnight staffing pattern, sleep-in to waking-night escalation protocol, eMAR-supported medication regime and incident reporting cycle into the answers themselves, with named roles, named systems and dated incidents, so that every operational claim was anchored to something that an inspector or a panel could verify if they chose to. The quality gate stage ran the full draft through a nineteen-check evaluator-style audit before submission, looking for plain English failure points, missing legislative references, any answer that did not directly close out an assessment criterion, any case example that was missing one of the five beats, and any policy citation that referenced superseded legislation. Three rewrites were carried out on that schedule before the upload itself.

Section 11How delivery was executed in practice

The submission described overnight short breaks delivered through a documented combination of waking-night and sleep-in staffing, with named escalation triggers that allowed the team to move between cover types when the assessment indicated that this was needed. Routine overnight checks were timed against the sleep-cycle pattern of each guest, and continence support, sensory-aware bedtime routines and quiet-time protocols protected the chance of restorative sleep across the night. Each guest had a one-page profile that summarised likes, dislikes, communication style and routine preferences, and a hospital passport ready to travel with the person if an unplanned admission became necessary.

For guests with behaviours that challenge, the response described positive behavioural support plans with antecedent-behaviour-consequence recording and a named protocol for de-escalation. Mental Capacity Act assessments and best-interest decisions were referenced for any restrictive practice, with the named senior staff member responsible for each, and morning re-engagement was structured as a documented handover rather than as an informal end of shift. The narrative did not over-claim. Where the Council's specification used a particular term, the response used that exact term, and every paragraph either closed an assessment criterion, named a system, named a role, or carried a dated example.

Section 12Key interventions that changed trajectory

Three interventions did the heavy lifting on the score, and each is repeatable on any adult social care DPS submission of this profile. The overnight staffing ratios moved to the front of the workforce section, so that the highest-weighted scoring point on the question was closed in the first paragraph, before the evaluator could form an early-read judgement on operational competence. This is a discipline that reads as obvious in retrospect and is consistently absent in submissions that lose marks at the workforce question.

Each case example was rewritten using a five-beat lessons-learned cycle, and the fifth beat, which documents how the lesson changed practice through a verifiable amendment to a named document, was given the same weight as the four beats that preceded it. Where there is no documented policy change, an evaluator concludes that the lesson did not change practice, and the case example loses its closing mark. Generic safeguarding language was replaced with named pathways into the Sheffield Safeguarding Adults Board, including referral routes, threshold guidance and the multi-agency response protocol, and the Mental Capacity Act narrative was anchored to the Council's expectation of supported decision-making rather than referenced as policy text.

Section 13Outcome achieved

The provisional award was confirmed on 24 January 2025 by Successful Alcatel Letter under Public Contract Regulations 2015 Regulation 87. Lot 3 Overnight Short Breaks was confirmed on the DPS, the standstill period commenced the following day, and at midnight on 3 February 2025 it closed without any challenge having been received. The DPS agreement then moved to execution through the Council's e-signing portal in line with the Council's standard procurement protocol.

Section 15Before and after position

Before the Engagement

The provider had no place on Sheffield's Adults with Disabilities DPS and no structured route to bid for Council overnight short breaks call-offs. The underlying overnight delivery was strong, but the procurement narrative that should have surfaced it was thin, and the operational facts that an evaluator needed to see were scattered across HR records, internal policy folders and the trainer's working knowledge rather than packaged for evaluator consumption. Submission discipline lived inside operational management rather than inside a dedicated bid function, and the response had not yet been read against a weighting register before drafting began.

After the Engagement

The provider holds a confirmed place on the DPS for Lot 3 and is eligible to receive every overnight short breaks call-off in Sheffield over the framework lifetime. The weighting register, the overnight staffing matrix and the five-beat case-example library all sit inside the organisation as reusable bid infrastructure, and they can be applied without rebuild on the next refresh and on adjacent procurements across South Yorkshire and West Yorkshire commissioning footprints.

Section 16Client confidence and capability post-engagement

The provider exited the engagement with three artefacts that did not exist as bid material at engagement start: a weighting register template, a populated case-example library, and a one-page overnight staffing matrix structured for evaluator consumption. The shift in confidence was structural rather than cosmetic. The procedural anxiety that had sat around portals, word counts and upload mechanics was replaced with operational confidence about which evidence wins which point, and the internal team can now read a specification like an evaluator and identify the assessment criteria, the named cohort terms and the hard gates inside the first hour of engagement on a new tender. The five-beat case-example structure is now the writing standard inside the organisation, applied across new bids and against existing answer-bank content.

Section 18Repeat or ongoing work

The DPS is live throughout its term, which means call-off opportunities will continue to arrive at irregular intervals and against varying lot scopes. The reusable infrastructure built during this engagement now forms the spine of every future Sheffield response and of related submissions across the South Yorkshire and West Yorkshire commissioning footprints. The provider has retained ongoing tender support on a call-off-by-call-off basis as opportunities are released through the Council's e-tender system, and adjacent local authority procurements with comparable overnight short breaks scope are being evaluated against the same evidence base before each submission begins.

Section 19Similar scenarios this applies to

The pattern recurs across adult social care DPS entries, regional short breaks tenders and refresh windows on existing frameworks. Operational maturity combined with bid-writing thinness is a common position for owner-led care companies, and it does not require structural change to fix; it requires evaluator-grade packaging of evidence the provider already holds.

  • Owner-led adult social care providers entering a Dynamic Purchasing System for the first time.
  • Operationally strong providers with thin internal bid writing capability and limited dedicated bid time.
  • Overnight short breaks, planned respite and carer break tenders across the North of England.
  • Sheffield, South Yorkshire and West Yorkshire commissioning portals running comparable DPS architectures.
  • Tenders requiring CQC registration, registered manager evidence and overnight staffing detail.
  • Bids exposed to PCR 2015 Regulation 87 challenge during the standstill window.

Section 20Outcome summary

This was a first-window DPS entry on a tight clock, with an operationally strong but procurement-thin provider. The win was driven by specification-led writing, an overnight-specific operational narrative, a five-beat case-example structure across every worked example and a Plain Meaning Grading audit before upload, and the provider walked away with a place on Sheffield City Council's Adults with Disabilities DPS for Lot 3 Overnight Short Breaks and with the bid infrastructure to keep that place defensible across the framework lifetime and across adjacent regional procurements.
