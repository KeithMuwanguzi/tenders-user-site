Section 01Case title

This case study examines a local authority Children's Services Care Packages procurement where the provider secured a position on the procurement at the documented lowest successful supplier threshold or above. The procurement applied a 0 to 5 scoring scale per question, with the score and the rationale awarded to the lowest ranking successful supplier published as the entry threshold for transparency. The published feedback ran to four pages and covered six scored questions in detail.

Section 02Organisation type and service setting

The Contracting Authority is a local authority Children's Services commissioning function procuring care packages for children and young people under the Children Act 1989, the Children and Families Act 2014 and Working Together to Safeguard Children. The setting includes community-based support and home-based care for children and young people, with a strong child-voice expectation built into the evaluation criteria.

The Authority's question architecture covered safeguarding (with sub-points addressing independence, emotional wellbeing and the voice of the child in care planning), recruitment (covering safeguarding training, supervision and staff wellbeing), care management (continuity, response to changes in need and communication with families and professionals), partnerships (with families, social workers, health, schools and other professionals), family engagement (focused on trust, flexibility, daily lives and family wellbeing), and a child-facing one-page profile evaluated by children and young people directly.

Section 03Client profile at entry

The provider arrived at engagement start as a care provider expanding into children's services from an operational base that included adult and community work. The bid history had not yet matured into children's-services-specific submission practice, and the internal team was confident on operational delivery but had not built children's-services-specific case examples or child-coproduced one-page profiles into the bid practice itself.

Children's services panels read every paragraph for cohort-specific signalling, and adult-derived content reads as competency risk on a children's services panel rather than as transferable experience. The provider therefore arrived with operational substance and a working draft that needed re-anchoring against the cohort the panel was actually evaluating.

Section 04Starting gaps and limitations

Three patterns showed up in the first read of the draft. The safeguarding content was correctly anchored to DSL identification, mandatory training, supervision and incident reporting, but it lacked child-specific operational depth, and the published feedback later recorded that the response provided moderate confidence and understanding that the service would be delivered to an acceptable standard. The care management content described continuity and review timings but did not yet cover communication directly with children and young people, and the published feedback later flagged this directly, noting that the response was clear on staffing structures and review cycles but less clear on how the provider would ensure communication with the child or young person.

The family engagement content described flexible support but did not cover how the provider would learn about the child as an individual to tailor care. The published feedback later recorded that the response included substantial information about adult care which was not relevant to the criteria, and the one-page profile lacked pictures and child-coproduced detail. The published feedback later flagged that the profile was simple and easy for young people to understand but had limited detail and not enough pictures.

Section 05Trigger for engagement

The provider needed a structured route to deliver care packages for the local authority's children and young people, and without a documented score profile against the Council's published evaluation criteria, the operational case for entry into children's services would not stand on first reading by an evaluator panel. The brief became to clear the procurement entry threshold with a documented evaluator score and to capture the published commentary as a direct improvement input for the next bid cycle.

Section 06Risk position at the outset

The risk was scoring at the floor across all six competencies. Children's services panels read for child-voice and family-individualisation cues that adult social care submissions routinely miss, and without rebuilding the case examples and the one-page profile around the cohort, the bid would have landed at the lowest acceptable threshold across multiple questions. The published feedback structure named the floor explicitly: the score and rationale awarded to the lowest ranking successful supplier are recorded for each of the six questions, which means the entry threshold per question is documented in the procurement record and below that threshold the bid is unsuccessful regardless of strength on other questions.

Section 07Constraints

The provider was working through six questions scored 0-5, with each question carrying multiple sub-points covering DSL identification, mandatory training, supervision, incident reporting, safe recruitment with multidisciplinary partners, training matrix, supervision and well-being, career progression, care package management, response to changes in need, communication with families and children and young people, partnership working with schools, families, social workers and health, family trust-building, and an accessible one-page profile with social story. The communication needs of the children and young people were built into the profile criteria explicitly, with the panel expecting plain language, photos or drawings, an explanation of who the provider is, what they do and how they help children, a quote from a child or young person who had been supported, and consideration of involving children and young people in creating or reviewing the profile.

Section 08Our role and level of involvement

We took the engagement as lead writer on every quality response. We owned the safeguarding mapping, the staffing case study, the partnership working narrative, the family engagement plan and the one-page profile structure, and we coordinated the upload and the Plain Meaning Grading audit before submission. The provider supplied operational facts: the 100 percent retention rate, multidisciplinary safe recruitment partnerships, named training matrix entries and the supervision cycle.

Section 09Stabilisation actions

Three stabilisation actions ran first. The safeguarding response was anchored in DSL identification, mandatory training, supervision, incident reporting and escalation through local safeguarding pathways. Staff recruitment was packaged around safe recruitment with multidisciplinary partners, a specialist training matrix, supervision, well-being and the 100 percent retention rate. Care package management was rebuilt around continuity, defined review timings and a structured response to changes in need.

The one-page profile was rewritten in plain language with visual support, designed to be evaluated by children and young people directly. The Authority's published criteria built in the communication needs of the children and young people the provider would be working with, and the family engagement narrative was rebuilt around individualised tailoring rather than generic flexibility, with a documented approach to learning about the child as an individual.

Section 10Approach to building capability

The submission led with safeguarding practice anchored in DSL identification, mandatory training, supervision and incident reporting through local safeguarding pathways. Staff recruitment was structured around safe recruitment, specialist training, supervision and the 100 percent retention rate, and care package management was wrapped in continuity, defined review timings and structured response to changes in need. Every paragraph was tested for cohort relevance, and adult-derived content was either rewritten to children's services context or removed.

The recruitment question carried the strongest evidence base on this submission, with multidisciplinary safe recruitment, specialist training, training matrix, supervision and the 100 percent retention rate all named and dated, and the result was a 4 of 5, the strongest score on the entire submission. The 3 of 5 and 2 of 5 questions were given documented improvement plans rather than over-claimed answers, because over-claiming on children's services panels exposes the bid to clarification correspondence that can dislodge other marks.

Section 11How delivery was executed in practice

The service was described as a child-centred care package operation with a designated key person leading family engagement, structured review cycles, multi-agency information sharing and clear escalation pathways, and family communication and trust-building were embedded across the package lifecycle. The published feedback recorded that the recruitment response demonstrated good examples of recruitment and safe recruitment strategies (involving multidisciplinary partners and family representatives), specialist training, training matrix, supervision and staff well-being support, career progression and development opportunities, and a 100 percent retention rate. The response accounted for staff supervision, although this could be further expanded to include CPD, staff team meeting and appraisals, which forms the next-cycle improvement carry-forward.

Section 12Key interventions that changed trajectory

Recruitment was the single biggest lift. The combination of multidisciplinary safe recruitment, a specialist training matrix, supervision, well-being and the 100 percent retention rate evidence carried Q2 to 4 of 5, the strongest response on the entire submission. Q1 Safeguarding, Q3 Care Management, Q4 Partnerships and Q5 Family Engagement landed at 3 of 5 with explicit improvement plans, and Q6 One-Page Profile gave the cleanest improvement signal for the next bid cycle, namely more pictures, more child-coproduced detail and more individual-child-focused content. The improvement carry-forward is now an explicit project: child voice as a structural element rather than a referenced concept, individualised family learning as a documented approach, and multi-agency partnerships at the level of named schools, named SENCos, named CAMHS pathways and named family advocacy organisations.

Section 13Outcome achieved

The provider was awarded a position on the procurement at the documented lowest successful supplier threshold or above. The strongest scoring response was Q2 Recruitment, Training and Retention at 4 of 5, with Q1, Q3, Q4 and Q5 at 3 of 5 and Q6 at 2 of 5.

Section 15Before and after position

Before the Engagement

The provider had no structured route to deliver care packages for the local authority's children and young people, and there was no documented score profile against the Council's published evaluation criteria. Adult social care narrative was bleeding into children's services answers, and the one-page profile lacked pictures and child-coproduced detail.

After the Engagement

The provider holds a position on the procurement, with a documented score profile across six competencies, a 4 of 5 result on recruitment, and clear improvement points captured directly from evaluator feedback for the next bid cycle. The children's services bid practice is now distinct from adult social care bid practice.

Section 16Client confidence and capability post-engagement

The provider now holds a children's-services case-example template, a child-coproduced one-page profile structure and a family engagement narrative built around individualised tailoring. The shift in confidence moved from carrying adult social care content into children's services bids to writing for the cohort the panel actually evaluates, and the improvement plan captured from the published feedback is being implemented in service: child voice structures, individualised family learning, multi-agency partnerships at named-organisation level, and CPD and appraisal cycles documented alongside supervision.

The response accounts for staff supervision although this could be further expanded to include CPD, staff team meeting and appraisals."

Section 18Repeat or ongoing work

The procurement remains live, and the improvement plan captured from this engagement feeds the next children's services bid cycle directly. The provider has retained ongoing support for adjacent children's procurements where similar question architectures operate, including SEND-aligned commissioning routes, and the cohort grounding discipline now embedded in the bid practice carries across every children's services submission rather than just the one that produced this scoreboard.

Section 19Similar scenarios this applies to

The pattern recurs across local authority children's services procurements where adult social care providers are stretching into children's services and where one-page profiles are evaluated by children and young people directly.

  • Local authority children's services care package procurements.
  • Tenders requiring DSL identification, mandatory safeguarding training and incident reporting evidence.
  • Submissions weighted on safe recruitment, training matrix, supervision and retention.
  • Procurements with mandatory child-facing one-page profiles and social stories.
  • Adult social care providers stretching into children's services.
  • Tenders requiring partnership working with schools, SENCos, CAMHS and family advocacy organisations.

Section 20Outcome summary

Awarded position on a children's services procurement with a clean documented score profile and a defined improvement plan against evaluator feedback. The strongest response was staff recruitment, training and retention at 4 of 5, and the provider's bid practice is now writing for the cohort the panel evaluates, with the published commentary feeding the next bid cycle as a direct improvement input.
