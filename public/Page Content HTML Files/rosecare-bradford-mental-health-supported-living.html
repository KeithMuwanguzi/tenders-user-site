Section 01Case title

This case study examines the route Rosecare Community Services Ltd took onto Bradford Council's Mental Health Supported Living Services Provider List, where the provider secured Lots 1 and 2 with six of eight technical responses at full marks. The procurement was run by the Council's Department of Adult Social Care, Commissioning and Contracting team based at Britannia House, Hall Ings, Bradford, under reference 85402, and the award letter reached the provider on 06 January 2025.

Section 02Organisation type and service setting

The Contracting Authority is Bradford Council, a metropolitan unitary authority commissioning mental health supported living through its Department of Adult Social Care, Commissioning and Contracting team. The procurement vehicle is a Provider List rather than a closed framework, which means successful providers receive Council placements at the Council's set rate in the pricing schedule, with individuals identified through Council review processes and any relevant updated information drawn from the Care and Support plan.

Lots 1 and 2 cover community-based supported accommodation for adults with primary mental health needs, with a service profile that supports recovery, reduces hospital admission and provides step-down from inpatient and crisis settings. The cohort scope includes adults with primary mental health diagnoses (psychosis, mood disorders and emerging or established personality disorders), adults stepping down from inpatient or rehabilitation services, adults with co-occurring substance misuse and mental health needs (dual diagnosis), adults with mental health needs and trauma backgrounds, and adults with mental health needs and learning disability or autism overlap.

Section 03Client profile at entry

Rosecare Community Services Ltd is an owner-led mental health supported living provider operating in West Yorkshire, with a recovery-focused, trauma-informed practice base. Operations were strong on the front line, but the submission discipline of writing every quality answer to a fixed two-mark rubric had not yet been built into the bid practice.

The 0-2 rubric is structurally distinct from a 0-5 banding because it collapses the gradient between 'good' and 'excellent' into a single binary mark. A response either reads to the rubric or it does not, with no half-credit for partial coverage, and most rejected mental health supported living submissions on this kind of rubric fail because the answer is written for a 0-5 banding instinct and lands in the middle of an unwritten gradient.

Section 04Starting gaps and limitations

Two structural gaps drove the lower scores in the published feedback. The Equality and Diversity content addressed accessible information and language-aware recruitment but did not yet address physical accessibility or demographically targeted recruitment to meet the demography of the district, and the Quality content covered audits, reviews and care planning but did not yet cover trends analysis on incidents, accidents and safeguarding. Both are recurring themes in mental health supported living submissions, and both were avoidable on a 0-2 rubric.

The two responses that scored 1 of 2 lost their second mark because the evaluator could not see the missing element on the page; the absence read as 'adequate' rather than 'full coverage', which on the rubric is the 1-point band. Below the named gaps, the submission needed to demonstrate coproduction at scale rather than reference it, with service user voice operational at committee and board level rather than mentioned as a value.

Section 05Trigger for engagement

The provider needed Lot 1 and Lot 2 acceptance to receive Council placements through the provider list and to deliver against the Council's pricing schedule. Without entry, the provider could not bid for any Bradford Council mental health supported living placement until the next refresh, and the provider list was scheduled to go operational on 27 January 2025, which meant readiness had to be locked in at the point of contract return.

Section 06Risk position at the outset

The headline risk was a partial entry, in which one lot was accepted and the other was declined, which would have split referral flow and complicated operational planning. The 0-2 rubric is binary in practice, with a 1 reading as adequate and a 2 reading as full marks, and drift between the two was the immediate cost driver across all eight questions. Below the partial-entry risk, the absence of trends analysis across incidents, accidents and safeguarding would weaken the next CQC inspection narrative as well as the next provider list refresh, so the improvement plan needed to be carried out in service rather than just documented in a bid file.

Section 07Constraints

The provider was working through eight technical questions, each scored 0-2, with submission via YORtender. The provider list was due to go operational just three weeks after award, and the pricing schedule was set by the Council, so commercial leverage was zero and the entire score had to come off quality. The signed contract was required for return by 15 January 2025, which left a tight window between award and operational readiness.

Section 08Our role and level of involvement

We took the engagement as end-to-end on quality. We owned every quality response, the trauma-informed care narrative, the safeguarding and Mental Capacity Act mapping, the workforce and recruitment evidence, and the vision and transformation positioning, and we coordinated the submission upload through YORtender. The provider supplied operational facts, named champion roles, recruitment partnerships and the recovery-focused operating model.

Section 09Stabilisation actions

Each response was anchored in a strengths-based, recovery-focused operating model. Trauma-Informed Care led with environmental factors, communication, peer support and staff training, supported by a dated case example. Safeguarding referenced legal framework, Mental Capacity Act and multi-agency working with the Council's local authority safeguarding pathways. Voice of People Supported referenced simple language, visual aids, committee involvement and board-level service user influence.

The 1-of-2 questions were given documented improvement plans rather than over-claimed answers. Q3 Equality and Diversity will need physical accessibility evidence and demographically targeted recruitment detail, and Q8 Quality will need trends analysis on incidents, accidents and safeguarding. These are now embedded improvements for the next bid cycle, being delivered in service rather than just held in the bid file.

Section 10Approach to building capability

Each answer was written to the 0-2 rubric with no filler and no hedging, and every paragraph closed an evaluator point. Where the rubric demanded a worked example, the answer carried a worked example. Where it demanded a system or a named role, the answer named the system and the role. The 0-2 rubric collapses gradient, so the discipline was to write to the higher band or accept the lower band cleanly, because there was no value in writing to a phantom middle band. Voice of People Supported was rebuilt around committee influence and board-level coproduction, with named structures and routes, since service user influence at board level is the difference between 1 and 2 on the published rubric.

Section 11How delivery was executed in practice

The service was described as a community-linked supported living offer with a named keyworker structure, recovery planning cycles, peer support arrangements, trauma-informed environments and risk-enabled goal setting. Multi-agency working was embedded in the safeguarding and quality narrative rather than referenced as an external concept.

The Council's evaluator commentary recorded that the response demonstrated a strengths-based recovery-focused approach, linking with the community and social activities and supported by a good example. Trauma-Informed Care was demonstrated with reference to environmental factors, communication, peer support and staff training, supported by a good example, and Safeguarding was anchored to the legal framework and Mental Capacity Act, with reference to a multi-agency approach and linking with the local authority, supported by a good example.

Section 12Key interventions that changed trajectory

Three interventions did the work. Trauma-Informed Care was rebuilt with environmental, communication, peer support and training detail and a dated case example, lifting it to full marks, with the published feedback recording no improvement points on this question. Voice of People Supported was rebuilt around committee influence and board-level coproduction, also lifting to full marks, and the Quality and Equality and Diversity responses were left at 1 of 2 with explicit improvement plans for the next bid cycle rather than over-claimed, because over-claiming on a binary rubric exposes the bid to clarification correspondence that can dislodge other marks.

Section 13Outcome achieved

Lots 1 and 2 were awarded, six of eight technical responses scored full marks (2/2), the provider list went operational on 27 January 2025, and Council placements began arriving at the agreed pricing schedule. The signed contract was returned via YORtender within the required nine-day window.

Section 15Before and after position

Before the Engagement

The provider was not on Bradford Council's Mental Health Supported Living Provider List and had no structured route to receive Council placements at the Council's set pricing schedule. Quality responses were written to a generic narrative rather than to a 0-2 rubric, coproduction was referenced rather than demonstrated, and trends analysis on incidents, accidents and safeguarding had not yet been packaged.

After the Engagement

The provider sits on Lots 1 and 2 with the provider list operational from 27 January 2025, six of eight responses at full marks, documented evaluator endorsement of trauma-informed care, recovery focus, safeguarding and coproduction, and a documented improvement plan for Equality and Diversity covering physical accessibility and targeted recruitment, alongside Quality covering trends analysis.

Section 16Client confidence and capability post-engagement

The provider now holds a 0-2 rubric template that reads correctly to evaluators on every mental health supported living procurement, and the shift in confidence moved from generalist quality writing to rubric-driven precision. The internal team can now read a Yorkshire and Humber commissioning specification and write to its rubric on first pass, Trauma-Informed Care is now demonstrated as a method rather than referenced as a concept, coproduction is now structured at committee and board level rather than described in narrative form, and the improvement plan for Q3 and Q8 is being delivered in service ahead of the next refresh.

Section 18Repeat or ongoing work

The provider list runs through the contract term. The 0-2 rubric template feeds adjacent mental health supported living submissions across the Yorkshire and Humber region, and the provider has retained ongoing tender support for related procurements, with the documented improvement plan being delivered in service ahead of the next refresh.

Section 19Similar scenarios this applies to

The pattern recurs across mental health supported living tenders, recovery-focused provider lists and binary-rubric submissions where coproduction depth is the marginal score driver.

  • Mental health supported living tenders and provider lists.
  • Trauma-informed care frameworks across recovery-focused services.
  • Local authority provider list refreshes with set pricing schedules.
  • Coproduction-weighted submissions requiring service user board influence.
  • Yorkshire and Humber metropolitan council mental health commissioning routes.
  • Tenders evaluated against 0-2 rubrics where binary precision drives the score.

Section 20Outcome summary

Two-lot win on Bradford Council's Mental Health Supported Living Provider List, with six of eight responses at full marks and the provider list operational from 27 January 2025 on the Council's pricing schedule. The provider now writes to the rubric rather than around it, with a documented improvement plan being delivered in service ahead of the next refresh.
